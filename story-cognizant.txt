THE ASCENT — CHAPTER ONE: COGNIZANT TECHNOLOGY SOLUTIONS
Program Manager / Enterprise Architect | September 2003 – April 2021 | India & USA
================================================================================

A note to the reader:
This is the first half of a twenty-three-year climb — the years when enterprise
scale, healthcare gravity, and the discipline of delivery shaped a developer into
an architect, a program manager, and a leader who never stopped writing code.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PROLOGUE — WHERE THE JOURNEY BEGINS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Every long ascent has a first foothold. Mine was not a boardroom or a product
launch stage — it was a terminal glow at Tidel Park, Chennai, in 2003.

I joined Cognizant Technology Solutions as a Programmer Analyst, stepping into
the world of core mainframe engineering: COBOL, DB2, IMS DB, CICS — the
bedrock languages of enterprise healthcare at scale. UnitedHealth Group was my
first client. I was not yet steering multi-million-dollar portfolios; I was
learning what it meant to make systems that could not afford to fail.

Those early years taught me something I have never unlearned: respect the
machine, respect the data, and respect the person on the other side of the
screen whose care depends on both.

[Client anchor: UnitedHealth Group · Chennai, India · 2003–2005 · Developer · Healthcare]


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1 — THE MAINFRAME CRADLE (2003–2005)
Chennai · UnitedHealth Group · Tidel Park
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Picture a young developer in a humming operations floor — green screens, batch
jobs, the quiet seriousness of healthcare IT. This was my classroom.

I lived inside the mainframe stack until it became second nature:

  • Programming: COBOL, JCL, CICS, REXX
  • Data & persistence: IMS DB/DC, DB2, VSAM
  • Environment & tooling: z/OS, RACF, Endevor, Xpeditor, FileAid

I was not observing enterprise systems from a distance — I was inside them,
debugging production flows, understanding how a single miscoded paragraph could
ripple across claims, eligibility, and member services.

Healthcare taught me precision before it taught me politics. Cognizant gave me
structure; UHG gave me purpose. Together they forged the craftsman I still am
today — the one who believes technology should never be a constraint, only a
canvas.

By 2005, I had earned something more valuable than a title: the confidence to
learn anything the next chapter demanded.

SECTION 2 — ACROSS THE ATLANTIC (2006–2012)
Hartford, Connecticut, USA · UnitedHealth Group
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In 2006, the journey crossed an ocean.

Hartford, Connecticut became home — and UnitedHealth Group became a masterclass
in growth. The healthcare industry was in motion: mergers, acquisitions,
integration programs, and the constant pressure to unify systems that were never
designed to speak the same language.

I arrived as an associate and left this chapter as a Project Manager — but the
real story is what happened in between.

I worked shoulder-to-shoulder with business and technology leaders through UHG's
M&A landscape, gradually expanding from hands-on development into system design,
delivery ownership, and client-facing program leadership. Mainframe depth
remained my foundation; .NET and Java entered the picture as the enterprise
stack widened. Program management became bread and butter — transparent with
the client, honest with leadership, simple with the team.

Thousands of miles from Chennai, I learned that trust is built in stand-ups,
status calls, and the moments you tell a stakeholder the truth before they ask
for it.

[Client anchor: UnitedHealth Group · Hartford, CT, USA · 2006–2012 · Lead Developer – Project Manager · Healthcare]

Technical threads woven in this era:
  Mainframe mastery (2003–2012, Expert) deepening into distributed enterprise
  delivery — the bridge from COBOL consoles to multi-tier healthcare platforms.

SECTION 3 — HOME AGAIN, LARGER HORIZONS (2012–2017)
Coimbatore, India · Offshore Delivery Leadership
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

By 2012, the compass pointed home — Coimbatore — but the responsibility had
grown far beyond a single desk.

I returned to India as offshore delivery lead at senior management level,
managing larger portfolios for UnitedHealth Group, Sanofi, and Abbott
Laboratories. The role was no longer "write the code" alone; it was orchestrate
the outcome across time zones, vendors, architects, and hundred-person rhythms.

For UHG, I carried forward years of institutional knowledge — now from the other
side of the partnership table: Solution Architect and Project Manager, ensuring
that what we promised in Hartford and Chennai would land with quality and
predictability.

For Abbott Laboratories (2019–2020 footprint begins in leadership maturity built
here) and Sanofi, life sciences brought new compliance rigor, new data models,
and new stakes — where a release window can touch research, manufacturing, and
patient-facing ecosystems.

SECTION 4 — THE ARCHITECT'S TURN (2018–2019)
EmblemHealth · Enterprise / Solution Architect
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In 2018, I deliberately changed altitude.

I moved into an Enterprise Architect role for a multi-year transformation program
with EmblemHealth — one of the most formative architectural chapters of my
Cognizant years.

This was not a single application upgrade. It was enterprise application
architecture at full span: roadmaps, reference models, integration patterns,
governance forums, and the painstaking alignment of business capability maps
with technical reality.

I learned to speak fluently to executives who needed outcomes, architects who
needed constraints, and engineers who needed clarity. Healthcare payer complexity
— products, networks, claims, provider data — demanded architecture that could
evolve without crumbling.

[Client anchor: EmblemHealth · Coimbatore · 2018–2019 · Enterprise / Solution Architect · Healthcare]

The technologist in me stayed hands-on: AWS Solution Architect certification,
cloud and hybrid architecture, full-stack sensibility, test-driven discipline.
The leader in me learned that architecture fails when it is slides without
sponsors — and succeeds when it is a story everyone can retell.

Keywords: Enterprise architecture · Digital transformation · Healthcare platforms
· Modernization · Quality engineering.

SECTION 5 — LIFE SCIENCES & THE INTELLIGENCE EDGE (2019–2021)
Abbott Laboratories · Sanofi
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The final Cognizant chapters carried me deeper into life sciences — and closer
to the intelligence systems that would define my next role.

**Abbott Laboratories (2019–2020) — Program Manager**
I led program management for large-scale delivery — modernization, integration,
quality engineering, and the unglamorous excellence of predictable releases.
Life sciences does not reward heroics; it rewards traceability, auditability, and
teams that can repeat success.

**Sanofi (2020–2021) — ML / NLP Solution Architect**
Here the arc bent toward the future. I applied machine learning and natural
language processing to real enterprise problems — Python ecosystems, NLP
libraries, document intelligence, and the emerging toolchain that would soon
explode into the GenAI era:

  Practitioner strengths: Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib, TensorFlow/Keras, Scikit-learn,
  SpaCy, NLTK, expert.ai, Tika, PDFPlumber, Tesseract, scispacy, BERT, pyTest

The coder was still alive. The architect was still curious. The program manager
still kept the trains on time.

EPILOGUE — WHAT COGNIZANT FORGED (2003–2021)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Eighteen years at Cognizant Technology Solutions were not a single job — they
were a curriculum written in production incidents, client trust, failed
deployments that taught more than successes, and teams that grew because
someone chose transparency over theatre.

**By the numbers (selected):**
  • 23+ years total career — Cognizant holds the first eighteen
  • $6M+ annual portfolio management
  • 100+ peak global team size
  • 25+ projects · 20+ RFPs won · Project of the Year recognition
  • Fortune 500 clients: UHG, EmblemHealth, Abbott, Sanofi

**Competencies hardened:**
  Technology Strategy & Roadmaps · Enterprise Architecture · Program & Portfolio
  Management · Healthcare & Life Sciences Platforms · Cloud & Hybrid Architecture
  · Machine Learning & NLP · Agile Delivery · Stakeholder Management · Team
  Building · Quality Engineering · Legacy-to-Cloud Modernization

**Technical compass at departure (Cognizant-era foundations + growth):**

  MAINFRAME (2003–2012, Expert): COBOL · JCL · CICS · REXX · IMS · DB2 · VSAM · z/OS

  ENTERPRISE & FULL STACK (2015–present, Expert/Practitioner):
    Python · Flask · Django · JavaScript · React · Vue · HTML/CSS/SASS
    SQL Server · MySQL · MongoDB · PostgreSQL · DynamoDB · RDS · Aurora · DocumentDB

  INTEGRATION & APIs: REST · SOAP · Django REST · AWS API Gateway · GraphQL ·
    Mulesoft · Informatica · Talend · Marklogic

  AWS (Practitioner): IAM · Cognito · EC2 · S3 · SQS · Lambda · Route 53 ·
    Lightsail · API Gateway · CloudWatch · CloudTrail · Comprehend · EFS

  MOBILE (Explorer): React Native · PhoneGap · Ionic · NativeScript

When I left in April 2021, I was not leaving the craft — I was carrying it to a
smaller hull with a bigger horizon: a startup SaaS product where I would build
the cloud from zero and, soon after, teach machines to think alongside strategists.

The developer at heart boarded the next ship.
