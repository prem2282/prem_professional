Reranking and Retrieval Quality

Vector search gave us speed. It did not give us truth at position one. Reranking is the second opinion—the expensive, slower pass that decides what the LLM actually reads.

In a recent retrieval postmortem, the bi-encoder had ranked a cheerful marketing blurb above the binding refund clause. The answer sounded authoritative because the model had context—just not the right context. We did not need a bigger embedding model. We needed a cross-encoder (or equivalent) to re-order the top 50 before packing context.

Four myths teams learn the hard way

Click any card to flip from the comfortable myth to what I see in production traces.

Myth

Reranking fixes bad chunking and stale indexes.

Reality

It reorders what you already retrieved. Garbage in the candidate pool stays garbage—you just might rank it lower, not eliminate it.

Myth

Higher top-K from vector search always helps reranking.

Reality

Very large K adds noise and latency. Most teams win with retrieve 30–80, rerank down to 5–12, then pack 3–6 for the LLM.

Myth

One reranker model works for every domain and language.

Reality

Legal, code, and multilingual corpora need evaluation per domain. A general cross-encoder can underperform on jargon-heavy internal wikis.

Myth

If MRR looks good offline, production answers will be fine.

Reality

Queries in prod drift. Measure nDCG@K, answer faithfulness, and "wrong doc at rank 1" rate on sampled live traffic.

The funnel mental model

Think of retrieval as a funnel, not a single shot. Stage one (bi-encoder / hybrid search) optimizes recall—get plausible candidates fast. Stage two (cross-encoder, LLM judge, or lightweight reranker) optimizes precision—put the one paragraph that answers the question at the top.

Corpus → retrieve K (50–100)
Rerank → top N (8–15)
Pack → context slots (3–6)

Production wiring (sketch)

Keep rerank behind a feature flag and log both orderings for sampled queries—your future self will thank you during regressions.

async def retrieve_and_rerank(query: str) -> list[Chunk]:
    candidates = await hybrid_search(query, k=50)  # dense + BM25
    if not candidates:
        return []

    if feature_flags.rerank_enabled:
        # Hosted or self-hosted cross-encoder
        ranked = await rerank_client.rank(
            query=query,
            documents=[c.text for c in candidates[:40]],
            top_n=12,
        )
        candidates = apply_scores(candidates, ranked)

    return pack_context(
        filter_acl_and_stale(candidates),
        max_chunks=4,
        max_tokens=6_000,
    )

Failure patterns I flag in reviews

  • Reranking the full index. Cross-encoders are pairwise-scoring machines, not search engines. Cap input to top-K from stage one.
  • Skipping rerank on "simple" queries. Simple wording often maps to ambiguous embeddings; that's when rerank pays most.
  • No score threshold. If nothing clears a minimum relevance score, return "I don't know" instead of stuffing weak chunks into the prompt.
  • Evaluating only on clean dev sets. Adversarial paraphrases and typos are where vector-only retrieval breaks first.
  • Ignoring freshness after rerank. Rerank by relevance, then apply recency or version filters before pack.

Operational thresholds that survive leadership reviews

  1. Track wrong document at rank 1 weekly on stratified live samples—not just average similarity.
  2. Alert when rerank latency p95 exceeds 2× baseline (GPU saturation or batch size drift).
  3. Re-run offline nDCG when you change chunking, embedding model, or ACL rules—rerank gains can vanish overnight.
  4. Cap rerank input; document the K→N→pack pipeline in your runbook so on-call does not guess.

Your turn

Export 200 production queries with human-labeled relevant chunk IDs. Plot vector-only vs reranked nDCG@3. If rerank moves fewer than 5% of queries materially, fix ingest and chunking before buying a heavier reranker.

Quick check

You retrieve 100 chunks and rerank all 100 with a cross-encoder on every query. What is the primary risk?

Latency and cost explosion—and little extra quality past ~30–40 candidates. Cross-encoders scale with pairs scored; stage one should narrow the field. You're paying for redundant scoring on chunks that never had a chance to reach the context window.
